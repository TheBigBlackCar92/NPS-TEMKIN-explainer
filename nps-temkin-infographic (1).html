Part 01 — Net Promoter Score

One question. A loyalty signal.

Introduced by Fred Reichheld in 2003 (Bain & Company / HBR). Customers rate on a 0–10 scale: 0–6 = Detractor, 7–8 = Passive, 9–10 = Promoter. NPS = %Promoters − %Detractors.

The Three Groups
SCORE 0–6 · DETRACTORS
Detractors

Unhappy customers who actively spread negative word-of-mouth. They slow growth and damage reputation.

"I contacted support 3 times and my issue is still unresolved. I would not recommend this to anyone."— Customer, telecom sector
SCORE 7–8 · PASSIVES
Passives

Satisfied but unenthusiastic. They switch easily to a competitor when a better offer comes along.

"It works fine, no complaints. Spectacular? Not really. I just use it."— Customer, software sector
SCORE 9–10 · PROMOTERS
Promoters

Loyal fans who actively recommend you. Your cheapest and most effective marketing channel.

"I have already referred at least 5 friends. Your service is truly exceptional."— Customer, e-commerce sector

Part 02 — Temkin Experience Ratings

Three dimensions. One complete picture.

Developed by Bruce Temkin (Qualtrics XM Institute). Where NPS measures loyalty, Temkin diagnoses why — across three equal dimensions, each scored on a 7-point scale.

The 7-Point Measurement Scale — How It Works

Unlike NPS which uses a 0–10 scale, each Temkin dimension is measured on a 7-point scale. The grouping mirrors NPS logic: scores 1–3 = Detractor, scores 4–5 = Passive, scores 6–7 = Promoter. The dimension score is %Promoters minus %Detractors — the "net goodness" percentage. Passives are excluded, exactly like in NPS. The overall Temkin Experience Rating is the simple average of the three dimension scores — no weighting applied.

All three dimensions carry equal weight — no weighting is applied. The methodology treats Success, Effort and Emotion as equally important parts of every customer experience.
Explore the Three Dimensions

"Did the experience meet your needs?"

Success measures whether customers accomplished what they came to do. The functional baseline — without it, no warmth or low friction compensates. Scored 1–7: 1–3 = Detractor, 4–5 = Passive, 6–7 = Promoter.

Why it matters
Even customers who feel positive about an interaction may churn if the core experience fails to deliver results. Success is non-negotiable before optimising Effort or Emotion.
Example
A customer tries to change their delivery address. Did they succeed first time? Or did it take 3 calls? That outcome — rated 1 to 7 — determines their Success score.

"How easy was it to accomplish your goal?"

Effort measures friction. Research shows reducing effort drives more loyalty than adding delight. High effort — even when successful — creates Detractors. Scored 1–7: 1–3 = high friction (Detractor), 4–5 = moderate (Passive), 6–7 = effortless (Promoter).

Why it matters
Customers experiencing high effort are far more likely to churn than those who simply lack a positive surprise. Fixing friction typically yields higher loyalty ROI than adding features.
Example
A complaint was resolved — but the customer called 3 times, re-explained each time, and waited 45 minutes on hold. Success ✓, but Effort score: 2 out of 7 (Detractor).

"How did the experience make you feel?"

Emotion captures the affective residue — how customers feel after engaging with you. Often the strongest loyalty predictor. Scored 1–7: 1–3 = negative (Detractor), 4–5 = neutral (Passive), 6–7 = positive (Promoter).

Why it matters
Memories drive future behaviour — not technical outcomes. A successful interaction that felt cold is remembered negatively. That memory determines whether the customer returns.
Example
"The agent solved my problem but was dismissive." Success ✓, Emotion: 2 out of 7 (Detractor). Counts negatively in Emotion net goodness despite a successful outcome.

Part 03 — NPS + Temkin Together

The compass & the map.

NPS (0–10) tells you where you stand on loyalty. Temkin (1–7, three dimensions) tells you why. Together they form a complete navigation system.

0.87
Pearson Correlation — NPS vs. Temkin Ratings
XM Institute (2023), 319 companies, 20 industries. Despite using different scales — NPS uses 0–10, Temkin uses 1–7 — the metrics are highly correlated but measure distinct aspects. They reinforce rather than replace each other.
🎯
NPS — The Loyalty Signal
0–10 scale · One question
  • Tracks loyalty trajectory with a single comparable number
  • Benchmarks externally against competitors and industry averages
  • Strongly correlated with churn, CLV, and word-of-mouth growth
  • High response rates — minimal survey fatigue
  • Cannot explain what is driving the score up or down
  • Culturally biased — scoring norms differ across countries
🔬
Temkin — The Diagnostic Layer
1–7 scale · Three equal dimensions
  • Pinpoints which dimension drives dissatisfaction
  • Enables targeted action — fix friction, retrain empathy, or fix product
  • Open standard benchmark — 300+ companies, 20 industries
  • More culturally resilient than NPS for cross-market comparison
  • Requires three questions — slightly more respondent effort
  • Fewer external benchmark datasets than NPS
Scenario Explorer — How They Work Together
NPS says
"Our NPS dropped from +42 to +34 this quarter."

NPS flags that loyalty has deteriorated — an 8-point drop is significant. But the 0–10 score alone cannot tell you whether the problem is product, process, or people.

Temkin adds

Temkin's 7-point scores show Success is stable, Effort improved, but the Emotion net goodness dropped 14 points. The problem is emotional — frontline behaviour or communication tone changed.

Action

Audit call recordings and chat transcripts. Identify whether a policy change or staffing shift triggered the Emotion drop. Target frontline empathy training specifically.

NPS says
"Our NPS of +38 matches the industry average."

Being average means no differentiation — customers have little reason to stay when a better offer comes along. But NPS cannot tell you where to act.

Temkin adds

Temkin shows your Effort net goodness is 18 points below the industry leader. Your Success and Emotion scores are competitive — it is specifically the friction in your journeys holding loyalty back.

Action

Set a 12-month goal to close the Effort gap. Audit the top 5 journeys by volume for friction. Present dimension benchmarks alongside NPS to show leadership where to invest.

NPS says
"We have budget for one major CX initiative. What do we prioritise?"

NPS by touchpoint shows post-purchase support scores lowest. But it cannot tell you whether to invest in technology, staffing, or process redesign.

Temkin adds

Temkin 7-point scores: Success 6.1 (Promoter zone), Effort 2.8 (Detractor zone — critical), Emotion 5.2 (Passive). The bottleneck is purely Effort — not capability or empathy.

Action

Invest in self-service tooling and intelligent routing — not headcount or training. Temkin diagnosis saves budget by ruling out wrong interventions. Target: contacts-per-resolution from 2.8 to 1.4.

NPS says
"Our Japanese market NPS of +12 is far below our US score of +56."

Raw NPS comparison across markets is misleading — Japanese consumers systematically score lower on 0–10 scales. Cultural calibration matters enormously with NPS.

Temkin adds

Temkin 7-point scores in Japan are above local benchmarks across all three dimensions. The NPS difference is a cultural artefact of the 0–10 scale — the experience is performing well.

Action

Benchmark NPS locally. Use Temkin net goodness scores for cross-market comparison — the 1–7 scale is more culturally resilient and produces comparable results across markets.

Part 04 — How to Use It

Six steps from measurement to action.

Measuring without acting is the most common CX programme failure. A practical playbook — with Temkin layered into each step.

01
🎯
Segment, Don't Average

Overall NPS hides real problems. Measure by product, region, touchpoint, and segment. Apply all three Temkin 7-point dimensions per segment for full diagnosis.

Chat NPS +62 vs. phone +18 → Temkin reveals whether it's an Effort or Emotion gap on the phone channel.
02
🔍
Mine the Open Answers

The NPS score says what — the open question says why. Map recurring themes to the three Temkin dimensions to prioritise fixes correctly.

Delivery time complaints → Effort. Dismissive tone → Emotion. Billing error → Success.
03
📞
Close the Loop — Fast

Contact Detractors within 48 hours. Thank Promoters. Ask Passives what would move them to a 6 or 7 on Temkin. Each conversation validates your dimension hypotheses.

Systematic follow-up converts 20–30% of Detractors within 90 days.
04
📈
Watch the Trend

One measurement says little. Track NPS and all three Temkin net goodness scores over time. Rising Emotion while Success stays flat is a very different story.

NPS +12 → +28 in 6 months after improving delivery — the Effort dimension drove the gain.
05
🔗
Link to Business Data

Connect NPS and Temkin scores to churn, CLV, and repeat purchase rates. Prove causation to build the business case for CX investment.

Promoters generate 3–4× higher lifetime value. Low Effort net goodness predicts 40% lower churn.
06
🏢
Make It Visible Everywhere

Share NPS and Temkin dashboards organisation-wide. Frontline teams with weekly visibility improve 2× faster than those receiving monthly reports.

Show each Temkin dimension per team: Success for product, Effort for ops, Emotion for service.
Part 05 — Pitfalls & Criticism

Powerful. Not perfect.

Understanding where these frameworks fall short is as important as knowing their strengths.

!
Measuring ≠ Acting

Collecting data monthly without structural changes is the single most common failure. The score changes nothing — only decisions do.

For every cycle, mandate an action plan for the lowest-performing Temkin dimension and the top 3 complaint drivers.
!
Gamification & Score Coaching

Employees evaluated on NPS may coach customers toward high scores. This corrupts data and makes trends meaningless.

Never tie NPS or Temkin to individual bonuses. Use team-level trends as indicators only.
!
Survivor Bias

Customers who churned don't fill in surveys. Both metrics over-represent customers who stayed — inflating scores artificially.

Combine with exit surveys and churn interviews. Churned customers provide the best Emotion dimension insight.
!
Cross-Cultural Comparison Errors

Japanese consumers rarely score 9–10 on NPS; Americans use extremes readily. A global dashboard treating all markets the same misrepresents performance. Temkin's 1–7 scale is more culturally resilient.

Benchmark NPS locally. Use Temkin net goodness scores for cross-market comparison.
!
Treating NPS as the Ultimate Metric

Companies with high NPS have failed; companies with low NPS have grown. NPS is one input into strategy — not a substitute for it.

Pair NPS with all three Temkin dimensions and a Customer Effort Score for a triangulated view.
!
Ignoring the Temkin Emotion Gap

Technical and B2B organisations routinely dismiss Emotion. It is consistently the strongest loyalty predictor even in engineering contexts.

Focus on eliminating negative emotional experiences (scores 1–3) before chasing positive ones. The floor matters more than the ceiling.
